Involvement in the Civil Rights Movement

Franklin was immersed in the civil rights movement for as long as she grew up with her father. She would express her support for the cause through multiple acts of service, such as providing funds for civil rights groups and performing at various protests. One of her most commendable acts during the civil rights movements was when she offered to post bail for Angela Davis, a prominent activist for civil rights who was jailed in 1970 after being accused of assisting in a courtroom siege that resulted in the deaths of four people and eluding the police.

At the time of her arrest, thousands of Americans across the nation began pushing for Davis' release from jail, attending rallies and protesting for her immediate release and freedom. Franklin also spoke up about Davis' imprisonment, declaring to the press that Davis deserves freedom, and that she would gladly pay for her bail, “whether it's $100,00 or $250,000.” While her father did not agree with her stance on the issue, Franklin told the press that although she still respects him and his ideals, she will nevertheless continue to stick to her beliefs and push for Davis' freedom.

“Angela Davis must go free. Black people will be free. I've been locked up (for disturbing the peace in Detroit) and I know you got to disturb the peace when you get no peace. Jail is hell to be in. I'm going to see her free if there is any justice in our courts, not because I believe in communism, but because she's a Black woman and she wants freedom for Black people. I have the money; I got it from Black people—they've made me financially able to have it—and I want to use it in ways that will help our people.”

The Meaning of “Respect”

Franklin's unwavering dedication and support for the civil rights movement extended far beyond her acts of good service and contributions to the community. Indeed, it would be impossible to talk about Aretha Franklin's involvement in the civil rights movement without mentioning her rendition of “Respect,” which is considered one of the most iconic and influential hits of her music career. The song was first recorded in 1965 by Otis Redding, an American singer and songwriter. The original meaning of the song was about a hard-working man requesting “a little respect” from his lover once he returns home from work.

Franklin, who was around 25 at the time, would later come across the song in 1967 and decided to transform it into that of her own, shifting the original point of view and giving it new meaning. In Franklin's rendition of the song, she outright demands respect from her lover rather than asking and pleading for it—to acknowledge all that she gives and all that she does for him. Her arrangement also involved spelling out the letters of the word, using her powerful, empowering vocals to drive home the song's memorable fifth verse alongside her backup singers. This was when “Respect” became an anthem for not just the civil rights movement, but a symbol of women's empowerment, too, proclaiming to the world that women deserved respect—especially women of color. In her autobiography, Franklin described how her version of “Respect” became a symbol to those who needed it.

“It was the need of a nation, the need of the average man and woman in the street, the businessman, the mother, the fireman, the teacher — everyone wanted respect. It was also one of the battle cries of the civil rights movement. The song took on monumental significance.”

'Respect' was more than just a song—it was a representation of the tumultuous times that African Americans endured throughout their lives. Although the fight for equal and civil rights was still going strong, segregation was still rampant and unrelenting, attempting to drown out the voices of protestors. But as people began to waver or lose courage to continue fighting for an equal nation, then came Aretha Franklin, who shattered the records with her voice. The song was nothing short of a masterpiece, becoming a hit on multiple radio stations and dominating the Billboard Pop Singles and Black Singles chart.

While African Americans were struggling—being disrespected and killed over expressing their want for civil rights—Franklin's song extended a hand to her people and brought them back up to their feet. 'Respect' became an anthem and a rallying cry for not just the civil rights movement, but a symbol of women's empowerment, too, proclaiming to the world that women deserve respect—especially women of color.
