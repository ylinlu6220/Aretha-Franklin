Involvement in the Women’s Rights Movement

Of course, the extent to which Aretha Franklin's voice revolutionized the world extended far beyond the reaches of the civil rights movement. 'Respect' transcended its role as an anthem for the civil rights movement; it emerged as a symbol of female empowerment, encapsulating the women's rights movements on a broader scale. Although Redding's initial recording of the song portrays the man as the pillar of the household, Franklin subverts the world's traditional gender norms by depicting the woman as the primary supporter of the household and her husband.

Another well-known song of Franklin's that also arose as a symbol of feminism and women's empowerment was 'Sisters Are Doin' It For Themselves,' which commended and celebrated the independence of women around the world when it hit the charts in 1985. Described as a modern feminist anthem, the song encourages women to rise above the societal norms placed upon them—that they were equal and capable of just as much as the men of their society were. With Annie Lennox and David A. Stewart, Franklin's vocals once again took the world by storm and brought the song to greater heights as a feminist anthem. The song's second verse is recognized for its appreciation of women from all walks of life—mothers and daughters, woman to woman, every single one of them deserved the same opportunities and respect.

“Now this is a song to celebrate the conscious liberation of the female state! Mothers, daughters, and their daughters, too. Woman to woman, we're singin' with you. The inferior sex got a new exterior—we got doctors, lawyers, politicians, too.”

'Sisters Are Doin' It For Themselves' did far more than just empower women and remind them of their value, of course; it went on to remind people around the world that women brought an undeniable strength and determination to every sphere of life that should be acknowledged in a more inclusive and equitable society.

“They say that 'it's a man's world,' but you can't prove that by me. But as long as we're together baby, show some respect for me.”

Yet another song made famous as a result of Franklin's talent was 'Do Right Woman, Do Right Man,' written by Chips Moman and Dan Penn. A country song that calls for respect for the woman in a relationship, Moman and Penn worked on the lyrics for the song prior to Franklin's arrival to the scene with Wexler. When the two presented the finished draft, Penn recalls Wexler telling them that something was missing—a bridge. Penn and Moman returned to the studio to work out the bridge and figure out what was missing before Wexler's next evaluation. Penn started writing down “They say that it's a man's world,” and Wexler would soon follow up with “but you can't prove that by me.” But what was it missing? Neither of them could figure out what the bridge needed to complete the song and send it off for recording. That was when Franklin chimed in with a line of her own: “But as long as we're together baby, show some respect for me.” And with that, the bridge—and the entire song, by extension—was complete.

However, recording the song in 1967 proved to be difficult for all members involved on the team. During one of the sessions, Franklin's husband and manager at the time, Ted White, engaged in an altercation with the trumpeter, Ken Laxton. The argument started at Rick Hall's FAME Studios in Alabama; although Rick Hall later tried to explain things and act as a mediator, his attempt resulted in a fight between him and White. The next morning, Franklin had abruptly disappeared from Alabama, leaving the song unfinished. For weeks, Wexler attempted to get back in contact with Franklin until she later reappeared and agreed to record once more, albeit this time in New York City. She was back on track—this time, without Ted White at the scene—and after the first few sessions, 'Do Right Woman, Do Right Man' was ready to be released to the public.

Along with her sisters, Erma and Carolyn, Franklin sang her heart out for another masterpiece of a song that emphasizes the need for equality between men and women. Women were “flesh and blood, just like a man,” and deserved to be treated as equals alongside men. If no one knew how to effectively convey the need for equality and respect in an era where women were lacking respect, then Franklin had just the words—and the voice—to proudly declare the message for them. And the song was a hit, with Rolling Stone listing the song as number 476 in the 500 Greatest Songs of All Time the year it was released. You could argue that no other female singer was as dedicated to the cause for equality as Aretha Franklin, and you could see her determination and passion in the lyrics she wove into the song herself, and many more to come.
