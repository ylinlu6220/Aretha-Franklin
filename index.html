Introduction to Arethra Franklin

Brought to you by: Alissa La'Shae Andrews, Kai Chavous, and Yu Lin Lu

Aretha Franklin, revered as the Queen of Soul, was more than just an iconic singer and songwriter—she was a civil rights activist whose outstanding vocal range resonated with people around the world, earning her a spot in the Rock & Roll Hall of Fame as its first female inductee. Twice acclaimed as the greatest singer of all time by Rolling Stone with global sales exceeding 75 million records, she indisputably stands as one of the world's best-selling music artists. But while her musical presence is widely regarded and still talked about today, Aretha Franklin's involvement and remarkable impact on the womens' rights and civil rights movement necessitates just as much recognition and discussion as her musical impact receives.

How exactly did she contribute to the fight for civil rights and women's rights? We see so much emphasis placed upon her musical career as the “Queen of Soul,” but her involvement in the civil rights and women's rights movements warrants a similar level of attention, too. And how did her most famous song, “Respect,” gain so much widespread fame and recognition—so much so, that it became an anthem representing the fight for equality for African Americans and women in America?

This project aims to discuss Aretha Franklin in depth, starting with her childhood and life before she rose to stardom before detailing how she came into the person we all know today—the revolutionary singer, songwriter, and performer whose voice changed the world. With her talents, Franklin used her gift to lead and perform work as a civil rights activist, becoming a memorable icon for women's rights in the process. The main goal of this project is to inform others of her work and most notable accomplishments, as well as how her actions impacted our lives today. You'll find that her work also is directly related to topics discussed in class, which we hope to further our research on—and learn more about who the legendary Aretha Franklin was, too.
